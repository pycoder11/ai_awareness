Evidence & Sources

Key claims and supporting evidence

Students using AI instead of learning
Survey responses and classroom observations show many students using AI to polish or produce assignments; anecdote from Green Level student who couldn’t explain their paper.
AI hallucinations spread misinformation
Scholarly and news sources (see Works Cited) indicate generative models sometimes invent facts and create convincing but false content.
Workplace automation and opacity
Reporting on algorithmic decision-making demonstrates risk of bias, lack of transparency, and lack of appeal.

Interviews (selected)

Murali — software industry veteran

Key point: AI speeds mundane work but requires technical expertise to filter errors. Concern: younger users may over-rely on AI and lose critical skills.

Ashwani — consultant

Key point: AI is useful for repetitive tasks and efficiency. Optimistic about students learning as before and using AI when needed.

Survey summary

~200 responses collected. Most use AI to save time (emails, research, study help). Majority see benefits, but many worry that AI could make users lazier or less skilled. A large minority believe the benefits outweigh downsides.

Local implication: majority opinion does not mean responsible use — it means policy and instruction are still necessary.
